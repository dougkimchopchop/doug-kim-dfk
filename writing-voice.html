Doug Kim/Your Writing Voice
Reverse-engineered from Istanbul, the film essay, and Tokyo

Your writing voice

Voice DNA
Rhythm
Short sentences land the punch. Long ones carry you somewhere. You alternate deliberately.
Entry point
Concrete image or scene first. Never an idea — always a thing.
The turn
Physical detail → interior thought. The ferry becomes the metaphor.
Profanity
Used surgically. It earns its keep because the sentences around it are clean.
Lists as music
Kadıköy, Karaköy, Ortaköy. Ueno, Taito, Edo. Rhythm breaks, not information.
The exit
End on image or action. Never on conclusion. Trust the reader.
Fragmented action
We advance the film. / We bow. / He nods. / We leave. Short lines as slow motion. For moments that earned it.
Direct address
Moon? Where are you? Talking to places, objects, the city. Intimacy without sentimentality.
Lyricism
You don't decorate sentences — you build them to sound like something. The engines thrum and hum and rock. Sound is structure.
Evoke the scene
Specific detail does the placing. You don't tell the reader where they are — you put them there. The sesame seeds. The wind. The Circassian girl gathering her hair.
Anti-overthink ritual — before you write
01
Read one paragraph you've written that felt right
Not to study it. To hear your cadence again. Istanbul works. The film rant works. Tokyo works. Thirty seconds.
02
Name the concrete thing you're starting from
Not your theme. A thing. A sound. A specific moment. One sentence, no editing. This is your first line or it earns your first line.
03
Write 200 words with no backspacing
Overthinking happens in the gap between writing and editing. Close the gap. Get the ugly first draft on the page before the editor wakes up.
04
Read it out loud once
Your ear catches what your eye misses. If a sentence stumbles when spoken, it's wrong. Your best lines have cadence you can feel in your mouth.
05
Find the sentence that sounds like a stranger
Read back and find the one sentence anyone could have written. Cut it or make it specific. That's where you left yourself.
Voice checklist — before you publish
Does it open on a thing, not an idea?
If your first sentence is abstract, find the image underneath it and start there.
Is there one line that could only be yours?
"The hills and I will not become friends." No one else wrote that. Find your equivalent in every piece.
Did you end on image or action?
If you ended by explaining what the piece meant, cut those last lines. The ferry closes itself. The film advances itself.
Is the irreverence earned?
Your wit lands because the writing around it is precise. If it feels forced, the sentences before it are probably soft.

Craft techniques — from Woolf's "Street Haunting"
Technique 01
The pretext object
Use a small, concrete object as the stated reason for the essay's journey — then let the real subject emerge underneath it. The object gives the piece permission to wander. It also provides a return: the essay closes on the pencil it opened with, and the circle is structural, not sentimental.
"Really I must buy a pencil" — the whole of London, the dwarf, the quarreling shopkeepers, the bookshop — all of it in service of a pencil that's really about freedom, self, and the pleasure of disappearing.
Your equivalent: the camera, the roll of film, the lens cap that never came off. An object that holds the essay's real weight.
Technique 02
General statement → specific scene → back out
Make a broad claim about human experience, then immediately ground it in a concrete scene that proves it, then return to the general. This is not abstraction — it's triangulation. The claim earns authority from the scene. The scene earns meaning from the claim. Neither works alone.
General: "We shed the self our friends know us by." Scene: the bowl from Mantua, the old woman, the quarreling innkeeper, the stars over the courtyard. Back out: all of it collapsing into that single moment "stamped like a coin."
Your version of this is the abstraction ladder — but Woolf shows you it must go both ways. Up to concept, down to thing, back up. Don't stay at either end.
Technique 03
The "we" that earns universality
Woolf's "we" is not a writing-workshop trick. It works because it comes after she has already established an intensely specific observer. The "we" only lands once the reader trusts the particular eye doing the seeing. Use "we" to invite the reader into an experience — not to avoid committing to your own.
Rule: the "we" must be purchased by specificity. You can't invite readers into an experience you haven't yet shown them.
Technique 04
The stranger as mirror
Drop a vivid stranger into the essay at its hinge point — someone observed briefly and precisely — whose situation illuminates the narrator's interior. Don't explain the stranger's significance. Render them so exactly that the significance arrives without announcement.
The dwarf enters the shoe shop. The ecstasy fades the moment she returns to the street. No commentary. The reader does the work.
You already do this — Farid in Morocco, the old man at the doorstep in Santiago. Trust it. Don't explain what these figures mean. Let them do it themselves.
Technique 05
The structural return
End where you began — not to summarize, but to show the distance traveled. The return is physical (same door, same chair, same object) but the reader arrives changed. The object that opened the essay reappears transformed. This is different from restating your thesis. The object does the work. You say nothing new.
"Here again is the usual door; here the chair turned as we left it and the china bowl and the brown ring on the carpet. And here... is the only spoil we have retrieved from all the treasures of the city, a lead pencil."
Let your object just sit there. Don't explain what it means.
A traveler's eye on the specific, a philosopher's instinct for what it means, and just enough irreverence to keep the philosophy honest.
