I Put the Cameras Down and Lost the World

I actually think you’ve found the tension that every memoir runs into.

The story isn’t about lockdown.
The story isn’t about Oaxaca.
The story isn’t about photography.

Those are simply the evidence.

The essay is trying to prove one sentence:

I forgot how to live long before I realized I had put the camera down.

Or perhaps even:

The camera wasn’t documenting my life. It was creating it.

Everything else has to earn its place by making that realization inevitable.

The mistake I think you’re making is believing you need to explain your entire life to justify the conclusion. You don’t. Readers don’t need your biography. They need to experience three or four moments that accumulate into the realization.

Think of it less like autobiography and more like a legal case.

Exhibit A

The silent street after returning from Oaxaca. The dead plant. The birds. The window.

Exhibit B

The corporate life shrinking the world to four blocks and a glowing screen.

Exhibit C

The Medellín realization that you’re repeating yourself.

Exhibit D

Years later, the world has reopened. The cameras are still on the shelf.

That’s enough.

Notice how little explanation is required.

···

The descriptions aren’t the burden.

They’re the reward.

The burden is the explanation that comes after them.

For example, instead of saying:

I was living two lives. One at work and one while traveling. I realized I only came alive abroad…

you could simply put two scenes next to each other.

Brooklyn. Slack notifications at 5:47 a.m. The same deli. The same subway entrance. Delivery containers stacked beside the sink.

Then:

Medellín. A pool hall. Men laughing because I knew one curse word. Walking twelve miles because laughter had come from somewhere around the next corner.

The reader concludes: these are two different people.

You don’t have to tell them.

···

There’s another thing I notice in your writing.

When you’re describing the world, you’re electric.

When you’re explaining yourself, the energy collapses.

For instance:

I touched one of the remaining green leaves and it crumbled like burnt paper.

That’s memorable.

Whereas:

I was thankful for the rest. I was repetitive. I wondered if I was running away.

Those are thoughts.

The leaf says more than the paragraph.

···

I wonder if the essay should be almost entirely scenes.

Imagine ending like this:

The world reopened.

Mine didn’t.

I still own the cameras.

I pick them up now and then.

But I haven’t really carried them.

The camera didn’t record my life.

It demanded one.

I put the cameras down.

Then I forgot to live.

No grand moral. Just the last piece clicking into place.

That feels truer to the strength of your writing. You’re at your best when you’re showing us a leaf turning to dust, an empty SoHo street, a pool hall in Medellín, or a silent apartment window. The lesson doesn’t need to be carried on your back—it emerges naturally if you choose the right scenes and trust the reader to connect them.
